## Literature review

### Influence of waiting behavior on passenger efficiency during boarding and alighting process on the metro platform

- A study from [Shen et al. (2026)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.simpat.2026.103260), using Chengdu Metro System in Chengdu, China as a case study.
- This study highlighted crowd density inside the carriage and waiting behavior on the platform which could also influence the boarding and alighting process.
- This study examined passenger's boarding and alighting efficiency through field survey and simulation study.
- The objective of this study: (1) to build and test a computer simulation model of the boarding and alighting process using real data collected from Chengdu Metro; (2) to study how different levels of crowding inside the train carriage affect how efficiently passengers can board and alight; and (3) to analyze how passengers waiting on the platform affect the time it takes for alighting passengers to walk from the train door to the stairs or escalators.

#### Methodology

- Using a <mark>modified Social Force Model</mark> to examine passenger movement characteristics (simulation) with anisotropic correction to the model, anisotropic means pedestrians react differently to things in front of them versus behind them.
- Shen et al. (2026) employed a modified Social Force Model (SFM) with four distinct agent types across two sub-models: **boarding passengers** and **alighting passengers** as active moving agents; **carriage standees** (inside the train) and **waiting pedestrians** (on the platform) as stationary agents with a desired speed of 0 m/s. While stationary agents do not self-propel, they are instantiated as discrete physical entities within the SFM, generating repulsive interaction forces that obstruct and deflect moving passengers.
- The use of Level of Service (LOS A–E) for carriage standees and crowd density (persons/m²) for platform waiting pedestrians serve as experimental design and reporting frameworks only — determining *how many* stationary agents are placed in the simulation — rather than as simulation mechanisms. The physical crowding effects are therefore produced agent-to-agent through the SFM's force equations, not by abstracting crowd density as a parameter.
- For validation, data collection was conducted with video cameras which were installed at Luomashi Station (selected station) to record the passenger boarding and alighting process between 3:00 PM and 5:00 PM.
- From video recordings, the survey obtained: (1) the passengers’ lining form; (2) the sequence rule of boarding and alighting groups; (3) the number of boarding and alighting passengers; (4) the boarding and alighting times.
- Additionally, the average boarding and alighting time, average boarding time, average alighting time, and the ratio of boarding and alighting passengers are calculated.
- 56 sets of data were observed as the dataset for further analysis.
- *Three passenger lining form*, when waiting on the platform, are classified: (1) lining up on one side (2) lining up on both sides (3) unrestricted lining form.
>*Passenger dwelling time* was introduced in this study, defined as the time spent since the opening of the train door to the time when the last alighting passenger arrives at the selected exit.
- Passenger dwelling time is crucial in measuring efficiency during alighting process.

#### Analysis

- The result of the study comprised in four scopes:
    -  Influence of lining form on passengers' boarding and alighting efficiency
    - Influence of sequence rule of boarding and alighting groups on passengers' boarding and alighting efficiency
    - Impact of carriage crowding density on passengers' boarding and alighting efficiency
    - Influence of waiting pedestrians on passenger efficiency on the platform

<u>Influence of lining form on passengers' boarding and alighting efficiency</u>

- Three core indicators were adopted for evaluation: (1) average boarding and alighting time (reflecting overall efficiency), (2) average alighting time (reflecting alighting-specific efficiency), (3) average boarding time (reflecting boarding-specific efficiency).
- **Lining up on one side produces the shortest overall boarding and alighting time**, making it the most efficient lining form in both the field survey and simulation. However, the differences between lining forms are very small (< 0.1 s), suggesting the practical impact is limited.
- **Unrestricted lining consistently leads to the longest alighting time**, as the lack of designated waiting areas creates more conflict and interference for passengers exiting the train.
- **Lining form has a statistically significant effect on alighting time** (field survey: p = 0.049; simulation: p < 0.001), confirming that how passengers queue genuinely affects alighting efficiency.
- **Lining form has no significant effect on-boarding time in real-world conditions** (field survey: p = 0.161), suggesting passengers adapt their boarding behavior more flexibly regardless of queue arrangement.
- **Lining strategy should match station demand** — lining up on both sides suits stations with high alighting demand, while unrestricted lining may be acceptable at low-volume stations.


<u>Influence of sequence rule of boarding and alighting groups on passengers' boarding and alighting efficiency</u>


- **The "alight first" rule significantly reduces alighting time**, with a reduction of 0.186 s (8.0%) in the field survey and 0.829 s (26.5%) in the simulation compared to the "board first" rule, confirming that letting passengers exit before others board improves alighting efficiency.
- **The "board first" rule produces a slightly shorter total boarding and alighting time**, but the difference is very small (0.006 s in field survey; 0.133 s in simulation) and not statistically significant (p > 0.05), meaning this advantage is practically negligible.
- **The "board first" rule causes greater instability in the alighting process**, as reflected by a much higher standard deviation in alighting time (1.421 s vs. 0.817 s in simulation). This suggests that prioritizing boarding passengers increases the risk of crowding and unpredictable delays for alighting passengers.
- **The "alight first" rule is recommended for most operational scenarios**, especially during peak hours and at busy transfer stations, where reducing alighting time and maintaining process stability are critical for safety and punctuality.
- **The "board first" rule may only be suitable in low-demand situations**, such as residential-area stations during off-peak hours, where alighting volume is low and the slight boarding time saving is more relevant.

<u>Impact of carriage crowding density on passengers' boarding and alighting efficiency</u>


Key Findings from Fig. 13 - Passengers' Alighting Time Under Different Crowd Density Inside the Carriage

- **Higher carriage density consistently slows down the alighting process.** As crowd density increases, total alighting time rises steadily, indicating that more crowded carriages make it harder for passengers to exit efficiently.
- **At LOS A–C (≤ 2.97 persons/m²), the impact remains small and manageable**, with only a modest increase of 7.9 s per 1 person/m², suggesting the alighting process is still relatively stable at these density levels.
- **LOS D (2.97 persons/m²) is a critical threshold.** A sharp jump in total alighting time is observed between LOS C and D, meaning that once this density is exceeded, alighting efficiency drops dramatically and rapidly.
- **Beyond LOS D, total alighting time more than doubles (+126.3%)**, with the deterioration rate accelerating to 6.7 times faster than at lower densities — even a small additional increase in crowding causes a disproportionately large delay.
- **Process stability also deteriorates beyond LOS D**, as the standard deviation rises sharply, making alighting times increasingly unpredictable and raising the risk of operational delays.

Key Findings from Fig. 14 — Passengers' Average Alighting Time Under Different Crowd Density Inside the Carriage

- **Individual alighting efficiency declines with rising carriage density**, reinforcing that crowding inside the carriage directly affects every passenger's ability to exit smoothly.
- **At LOS A–C, each passenger alights efficiently at around 2.12 s/person**, with minimal variation, indicating a stable and comfortable alighting environment.
- **Beyond LOS D, individual alighting time surges by 130.7% to 4.89 s/person**, with the rate of increase becoming 5.6 times faster — confirming that crossing this threshold severely harms individual alighting performance.
- **Individual alighting times become highly unpredictable beyond LOS D**, with the standard deviation exceeding 4 s at LOS E, meaning some passengers face extremely long waits while others do not.
- **More boarding passengers make alighting even harder**, as increasing boarding passengers from 5 to 20 raises the average alighting time from 3.98 to 5.76 s/person at LOS D and above, highlighting that managing both passenger flows together is essential.

Fig. 15 — Passengers' Boarding Time Under Different Crowd Density Inside the Carriage

- **Higher carriage density consistently slows down the boarding process**, with total boarding time rising steadily as density increases, showing that crowded carriages make it harder for passengers to enter efficiently.
- **At LOS A–C (≤ 2.97 persons/m²), the impact on boarding remains moderate**, with an average total boarding time of 47.95 s and a marginal increment of only 7.2 s per 1 person/m², suggesting the boarding process is still stable and manageable at these density levels.
- **LOS D acts as a critical turning point for boarding efficiency.** A sharp rise in total boarding time is observed between LOS C and D, confirming that exceeding this density threshold causes a sudden and significant deterioration in boarding performance.
- **Beyond LOS D, total boarding time more than doubles (+111.8%)**, reaching 101.58 s on average, with the marginal increment accelerating to 49.8 s per 1 person/m² — 6.9 times higher than at LOS A–C, meaning that even slight additional crowding causes disproportionately large boarding delays.
- **Boarding stability breaks down dramatically beyond LOS D.** The standard deviation of total boarding time jumps from around 1.8–2.0 s at LOS A–C to over 10 s at LOS D and exceeding 20 s at LOS E, indicating that the boarding process becomes far more unpredictable and prone to unexpected delays under high-density conditions — even more so than the alighting process.

Key Findings: Table 8 & Figure 18 — Shortest Dwelling Time of Alighting Passengers

- Waiting pedestrians always slow down even the fastest passengers. Regardless of how many passengers are alighting, the shortest dwelling time rises as more people wait on the platform — meaning no alighting passenger escapes the crowd effect entirely.
- More alighting passengers = shorter fastest escape time. At any fixed waiting crowd size, shortest dwelling time decreases as alighting volume increases (e.g., at 2,000 waiting pedestrians: 23.15 s for 100 pax vs. 16.40 s for 500 pax). Larger alighting groups naturally carve pathways through stationary crowds more efficiently.
- Absolute delays are small but cumulatively costly. The maximum added delay for the fastest passenger is only ~7.25 s — but scaled across 32 doors, 20 trains/hour, and thousands of daily passengers, the aggregate impact on platform clearance and dwell-time management is operationally significant

Key Findings: Table 9 & Figure 19 - Longest Dwelling Time of Alighting Passengers
- Longest dwelling time consistently rises with more waiting pedestrians. The authors confirm a clear upward trend across all alighting volumes in Fig. 19 — waiting crowds on the platform directly and reliably worsen overall efficiency, with no exception observed.
- The increments are large and practically significant. Authors report that across 100–500 alighting passengers/train, the longest dwelling time increases by 100.7 s to 112.55 s (absolute) when comparing 0 vs. 2,000 waiting pedestrians — translating to 58.6–71.8% relative increases. These are far larger than the effects seen on the fastest passengers, underscoring how the last passengers bear the bulk of the crowd burden.
- Fig. 19 shows two distinct behavioral phases. The authors explicitly identify a linear phase (up to ~1,600 waiting pedestrians, density < 1.088 persons/m²) where dwelling time grows steadily, followed by a non-linear acceleration beyond 1,600, where the resistance from waiting pedestrians becomes "increasingly obvious." Each additional pedestrian beyond this point causes disproportionately greater delays.
- Alighting passengers are forced to detour and stop. The authors explain this through the Social Force Model logic: waiting pedestrians exert a "repulsive force" that pushes alighting passengers off direct paths, extending their walking distance and sometimes forcing them to slow down or stop entirely to find a new route — especially in high-density zones near train doors and stair-escalator groups.
- The slowest passengers travel farther and face more interference. Authors note that the fastest passengers typically use the nearest exit (short path, few obstacles), while the slowest must travel to farther exits — encountering more waiting pedestrians, more alighting peers, and longer exposure to compounding repulsive forces. This explains why the longest dwelling time is so much more sensitive to waiting crowds than the shortest.
- More alighting passengers amplify the delay for the slowest. The authors observe that as alighting volume increases, interactions among alighting passengers themselves add another layer of friction on the way to exits — meaning greater alighting demand both helps the fast movers and hinders the slow ones.
- The 71.8% worst-case increase (100 pax/train) reveals a counterintuitive vulnerability. Quieter trains — which operators might assume are "easy" to manage — actually produce the most vulnerable alighting passengers. With fewer people creating natural pathways through the crowd, individual passengers are more isolated and more easily blocked.
- Table 9 exposes an asymmetry: waiting crowds hurt clearance far more than they hurt entry speed. Combined with Table 8, it is clear that while waiting crowds add at most ~7 s to the fastest passenger's exit, they add over 100 s to the slowest's. This asymmetry means that conventional efficiency metrics focused on average or fastest times will underestimate the true congestion impact on platform operations.

Key finding from Figure 20
- More waiting pedestrians = longer, more twisted paths. As waiting crowd size grows from Fig. 20a to 20e, alighting passengers' routes become visibly longer and more winding. What should be a near-straight walk to the exit turns into a series of detours around stationary people.
- Waiting pedestrians act as physical barriers. Stationary crowds generate a "repulsive force" that pushes alighting passengers sideways and off their intended routes. This forces unnecessary extra walking distance and reduces exit speed for everyone.
- Fast passengers take short paths; slow passengers take long ones. The quickest exiters head straight to the nearest exit — short distance, fewer obstacles. The slowest must travel farther across the platform, passing through more waiting pedestrians and more fellow alighting passengers, compounding every delay along the way.
- Alighting passengers also interfere with each other. Beyond the waiting crowd, alighting passengers themselves create mutual friction as they converge toward exits. This peer-to-peer interference, combined with the waiting crowd effect, produces the increasingly unpredictable and prolonged clearance times reported in Table 9
- The trajectory evidence directly explains the dwelling time data. Fig. 20 provides the visual mechanism behind Tables 8 and 9 — the longer and more circuitous the path, the greater the dwelling time, and this relationship worsens non-linearly as crowd density rises.

### A study on passengers' alighting and boarding process at metro platform by computer simulation

- A study from [Li Z. et al. (2020)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tra.2019.12.017)
- Objective of the study: present a microscopic simulation model to simulate the metro passengers’ alighting and boarding process and analyze the impacts that passengers’ non-compliance behaviors have on the alighting and boarding duration
- **A gap in prior research**: The authors note that most existing studies on alighting/boarding processes mainly focus on infrastructure and design factors (e.g., door width, number of doors) to improve efficiency and reduce dwell time, while few studies examine the effects of passengers' own autonomous behaviors and the alighting/boarding rules themselves.
- **Defining the behavior**: Most metro stations promote the rule "Let passengers get off first then get aboard." However, the authors observe that boarding passengers sometimes start boarding while several alighting passengers are still inside the car. The authors define this as "non-compliance behavior" and note that it has been largely omitted in previous research.
- **Why it matters operationally**: When non-compliance occurs, the narrow metro door effectively becomes a bottleneck, since alighting and boarding flows move in conflicting directions through the same space. This means the resulting alighting/boarding duration differs from the idealized "alight first, then board" scenario.
- **An open question motivating the study**: The authors frame it as an open, interesting question whether non-compliance behavior makes the overall alighting and boarding duration longer or shorter, and note that this outcome may depend on passenger volume conditions — which is what their study sets out to investigate through simulation.
- **Direct link to the study's aim**: The introduction explicitly states that the study's goal is to build a microscopic simulation model of the alighting/boarding process and use it to analyze the impacts of passengers' non-compliance behaviors on alighting and boarding duration.

#### Methodology

<u>Data collection</u>
- Data were collected through field survey in a densely populated metro station at downtown area in Hongkong (only one station).
- Using cameras to record metro passengers boarding and alighting process during non-peak time (weekday from 11 am to 2 pm)
- The reason this study chose non-peak time too many or too few alighting and boarding passengers at peak or idle time would lead to data distortion, which cannot reflect passenger’s alighting and boarding characteristics in normal circumstance.
- Alighting and boarding efficiency indicators were prepared shown in table below:



| Symbol         | Definition                                                                 |
|-----------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| $T_a$          | Alighting duration (s)                                                     |
| $T_b$          | Boarding duration (s)                                                      |
| $T_t$          | Total duration of alighting and boarding (s)                              |
| $T_{ab}$       | Duration of simultaneously alighting and boarding (s)                     |
| $N_a$          | Number of alighting passengers through a certain metro door               |
| $N_b$          | Number of boarding passengers through a certain metro door                |
| $N_r$          | Number of remaining alighting passengers when boarding movement starts    |
| $T_{a\_ave}$   | Average alighting duration per passenger (s/pass), $T_{a\_ave} = T_a / N_a$ |
| $T_{b\_ave}$   | Average boarding duration per passenger (s/pass), $T_{b\_ave} = T_b / N_b$ |
| $T_{t\_ave}$   | Average total duration per passenger (s/pass), $T_{t\_ave} = T_t / (N_a + N_b)$ |

- 80 sets of valid data obtained through field survey.

<u>Analysis</u>

- Using Anylogic software
- Simulation of passengers dynamics by using Social Force Model based on [Helbing & Molnár (1995)](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevE.51.4282) and [Helbing, Farkas & Vicsek (2000)](https://doi.org/10.1038/35035023).
- This study classified passengers into three types: alighting passengers, boarding passengers, and passenger staying in metro carriage.
- Alighting passengers logic:
  - Start in the interior waiting area and head to the **nearest door** rather than a fixed assigned door.
  - Queue and wait inside the carriage until the door opens.
  - Once the door opens, they pass through the door line onto the platform.
  - After reaching the platform, they proceed to the platform sink line and exit the simulation.
- Boarding passengers logic:
  - Initially queue at platform waiting lines closest to their boarding door (capacity capped at 3 passengers per line).
  - Additional passengers beyond that capacity wait in the broader platform waiting area.
  - Boarding is conditional, not immediate: passengers only start entering once the number of remaining alighting passengers inside drops to or below a threshold value, $N_r$.
  - After entering the interior zone, they move to the interior waiting area.
- Staying passengers logic:
  - Generated at the interior source line (already inside the carriage).
  - Prioritize taking an empty seat if one is available.
  - Once seats are full, remaining staying passengers choose random standing locations.
  - They make small positional adjustments to give way when alighting or boarding passengers pass nearby.


<u>Findings</u>

- Finding are comprised into three scenarios, depend on the number of staying passengers:
  - Scenario of unoccupied environment (number of staying passenger = 0)
  - Scenario of normal environment (number of staying passengers = 60)
  - Scenario of crowded environment (number of staying passenger 100)
- $N_r$ is set from 0 to 10 in each scenario (non-compliance behavior).
- Result when **unoccupied environment** (number of staying passenger = 0)
  - Alighting time ($T_a$) and boarding time ($T_b$) are increasing with the growth of ($N_r$), while total duration ($T_t$) seems to be stable with the increase of ($N_r$)
  - **When people don't wait their turn, it slows them down individually — but barely affects the group as a whole.** As more people boarded before alighting passengers finished getting off, both groups took longer to get through the door. But this didn't really change how long the whole process took overall.
  - **The most interesting result: total time stayed about the same no matter how much overlap there was.** Even though individual alighting and boarding got slower, letting people move in both directions at once seemed to cancel out that slowdown. So overall, nobody was really losing time as a group.
  - **This flat result is a real pattern, not just random noise.** The data clearly showed no meaningful link between overlap level and total time — a different and weaker pattern than what was seen for alighting and boarding on their own.
  - **In plain terms: when the train is empty, letting people board before others get off doesn't really hurt overall speed.** So the paper's advice to still follow "alight first, then board" in this case isn't about saving time — it's more about avoiding crowding and keeping things smooth for each person, since there isn't much overall speed to gain anyway.
  - **This result also sets up a contrast for later sections.** It shows that overlap only becomes a real problem once there are other passengers already inside the train, which is what the next sections (normal and crowded scenarios) go on to show.
- Result when **normal environment** (number of staying passenger = 80 $\rightarrow$ sitting = 48; standing passenger = 32)
  - **A little rule-breaking isn't automatically a bad thing here.** Just like before, both alighting and boarding got slower the more people overlapped. But unlike the empty-carriage case, the overall time didn't just stay flat — it actually formed a "U shape," dropping at first and then rising later.
  - **Small amounts of overlap actually helped speed things up.** When only a few people boarded early, it didn't add much extra trouble, because the passengers already standing inside the train were already the bigger source of delay. Letting a little overlap happen let both groups move at the same time, which saved time overall.
  - **Too much overlap eventually backfires.** Once too many people started boarding before others got off, the crowding got serious enough that moving in both directions at once became more of a problem than a help. At that point, the time "saved" by overlapping could no longer make up for the extra slowdown it caused, so total time started climbing again.
  - **Alighting suffered a bit more than boarding.** Getting off proved slightly more sensitive to the crowd inside, since everyone leaving has to funnel toward one specific door, while people boarding can spread out to different spots once inside. This made alighting times a bit less predictable than boarding times.
  - **The practical lesson: a little flexibility is fine, but there's a tipping point.** For a normally busy train, it's best to let most alighting passengers get off first, then start boarding — allowing a small overlap can help, but letting too many people rush in early ends up slowing everyone down instead.
- Result when **crowded environment** (number of staying passenger = 100 $\rightarrow$ sitting = 48; standing passenger = 52)
  - **In a packed train, breaking the "wait your turn" rule really backfires.** Just like in the other scenarios, both alighting and boarding got slower as more overlap happened. But here, unlike the empty or normal carriage, the total time didn't level off or dip — it just kept climbing.
  - **The more people cut in line, the worse it got — consistently.** As the overlap level increased from none to the highest level tested, total time went up by roughly 30%. This wasn't a small or shaky trend — the data showed a strong, clear, steady increase.
  - **The reason comes down to too much resistance in the crowd.** With so many passengers already packed inside the train, letting people move in both directions at once no longer helps things move faster. Instead, the crowding is so severe that the "shortcut" benefit of overlapping movement (seen in the normal scenario) disappears entirely — overlapping just adds more congestion on top of an already slow process.
  - **The practical lesson here is the clearest of all three scenarios: strict turn-taking matters most when it's crowded.** In a packed train, it's best to have boarding passengers wait until everyone has gotten off before starting to board. This isn't just a minor efficiency tip in this case — skipping it noticeably slows down the whole process for everyone.
