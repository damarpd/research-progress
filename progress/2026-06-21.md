### Measuring rail passenger crowding: Scale development and psychometric properties

- A study from [Mahudin et al, (2012)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.trf.2011.11.006)
- From this study, the authors took definition regarding density and crowding from [Stokols, (1972)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar_lookup?title=On%20the%20distinction%20between%20density%20and%20crowding%3A%20some%20implications%20for%20further%20research&publication_year=1972&author=D.%20Stokols), which argued that density could be defined as a physical condition of having limited space available for the given number of individuals present, is considered as a necessary but not sufficient condition for crowding, whereas crowding is defined as a subjective, psychological state in which one’s expectation of space exceeds the available supply.
- This study aims to develop and test an instrument designed to tap the different psychological components of crowding
- Contribution of this study comprises into threefold:

| No. | Purpose / Contribution | Description |
|-----|------------------------|-------------|
| 1 | Theory | The development of the instrument could clarify and make more concrete the meaning of rail passenger crowding, thus providing a specific operational definition of the construct. |
| 2 | Support Future Research | A reliable and valid instrument with good psychometric properties could lend itself to future research in the field. |
| 3 | Practical | An instrument that adequately reflects the experience of crowding among rail users could be used as an assessment tool to aid train operating companies as well as relevant authorities in addressing the issue of passenger crowding. |

- Alongside the three latent construct, the study argued the need for retaining passenger density (an objective measurement usually utilized by public transport operator)
- The reason behind, because it is theoretically distinct from perceived crowding yet empirically related to it, plays a unique explanatory role in shaping affective reactions to crowding, and must be included to fully capture the interplay among all components of the crowding experience and their collective effects on passenger outcomes such as stress and exhaustion.
- First, respondents were presented with a scale made up of four pictorial representations of commuters of increasing passenger density developed by UK Rail Safety and Standard Board
- The outcome of the model was <mark>stress</mark> and <mark>feeling exhausted</mark>.


#### Findings
- The study proposed three-nested model comprising Model 1, Model 2, and Model 3 which can be defined as shown below. 

| Feature | Model 1 (Unconstrained) | Model 2 (Direct Path) | Model 3 (Hypothesized / Mediation) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | A baseline model where all paths among variables are freely estimated with no constraints. | A model that includes only direct paths from the three crowding predictors and passenger density to the outcomes (stress and exhaustion), with affective reactions removed. | The theoretically proposed model where affective reactions to crowding fully mediate the relationships between the predictors and outcomes. All direct paths from predictors to outcomes are constrained to zero. |
| **Paths from predictors to outcomes (stress & exhaustion)** | Free to vary (estimated freely) | Included and free to vary | Constrained to zero (no direct effects) |
| **Paths to/from affective reactions** | Free to vary (estimated freely) | Constrained to zero (affective reactions removed from model) | Free to vary (affective reactions act as mediator) |
| **Role of affective reactions** | Estimated freely alongside direct paths | Not included / suppressed | Central mediating variable between predictors and outcomes |
| **Predictors included** | Evaluation of psychosocial aspects, ambient environment, and rated passenger density | Evaluation of psychosocial aspects, ambient environment, and rated passenger density | Evaluation of psychosocial aspects, ambient environment, and rated passenger density |
| **Key purpose** | Serves as a reference/baseline to compare all other models | Tests whether crowding predictors have significant *direct* effects on stress and exhaustion, bypassing affective reactions | Tests whether affective reactions *fully mediate* the relationship between crowding and negative outcomes |

- Evaluation of psychosocial aspects significantly predicts affective reactions ($\beta = .51$, $p = .002$), indicating that negative perceptions of the social dynamics of the crowded situation strongly drive emotional crowding responses.
- Ambient environment evaluation significantly predicts affective reactions ($\beta = .36$, $p = .003$), showing that negative perceptions of physical conditions (e.g., heat, smell, noise, stuffiness) also contribute to affective feelings of crowdedness.
- Rated passenger density significantly predicts affective reactions ($\beta = .13$, $p = .001$), though its effect is notably smaller compared to the two psychological evaluation components.
- Combined, these three predictors explain 79% of the variance in affective reactions ($R^2 = .79$).
- Evaluation of psychosocial aspects $\rightarrow$ Stress: $\beta = .03$, $p = .643$ (not significant)
- Ambient environment $\rightarrow$ Stress: $\beta = .09$, $p = .100$ (not significant)
- Rated passenger density $\rightarrow$ Stress: $\beta = .07$, $p = .146$ (not significant)
- Evaluation of psychosocial aspects $\rightarrow$ Exhaustion: $\beta = .05$, $p = .528$ (not significant)
- Ambient environment $\rightarrow$ Exhaustion: $\beta = .05$, $p = .434$ (not significant)
- Rated passenger density $\rightarrow$ Exhaustion: $\beta = -.08$, $p = .234$ (not significant)
- Affective reactions significantly predict stress ($\beta = .47$, $p = .002$), explaining 38% of its variance ($R^2 = .38$).
- Affective reactions predict feelings of exhaustion ($\beta = .18$, $p = .072$), explaining only 5% of its variance ($R^2 = .05$), though this path is marginally non-significant at the $.05$ level.
- None of the three predictors — psychosocial evaluation, ambient environment, or rated passenger density — have a statistically significant direct effect on stress or exhaustion (all $p > .05$).
- The effects of all predictors on negative outcomes are fully channelled through affective reactions to crowding, confirming affective reactions as the key mediating mechanism in this model.

### Models of bus boarding and alighting dynamics

- Dwell time elaboration was delivered by [Sun et al. 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tra.2014.09.007), a study from Singapore using individual smart-card data from automated fare collection (AFC):
    - Dwell time is the time in which a public transport vehicle is stopped to transfer passengers, including the time necessary to open and close doors, plus any other time in which a bus has the doors open without passenger service. 
    - Dwell time starts with the opening and ends with the closing of bus doors, allowing passengers to board and alight. 
    - Dwell time may cover a great proportion of total travel time, which shows the significance of boarding and alighting processes on bus operation
- The authors argued that the contribution of this study indicating that, to date, there is no detailed analysis of boarding and alighting behavior at a microscopic passenger-to-passenger level, for which a system based on smart card transactions, is well suited as the exact time in which a person taps on and off is recorded, then passenger intervals are instantly recorded.
- Therefore, new opportunities to study boarding/alighting dynamics and dwell time at a microscopic level arise from the presence of smart card based automated fare collection systems.

#### Finding correlating to bus model configuration and boarding time

- Bus configurations with slower boarding times tend to exhibit higher variability in boarding intervals.
- Greater boarding variability leads directly to increased variability in travel times.
- High travel time variability produces undesirable effects on bus operations, making scheduling and service management more difficult.
- From the passenger's perspective, such variability is negatively valued, as riders prefer predictable and reliable travel times.
- **Regression result**: Higher on-board occupancy before the stop significantly increases boarding time, adding 0.340 seconds per passenger per unit of occupancy. This reflects in-vehicle friction — when the bus is already crowded, boarding passengers face more resistance navigating to their standing/seating position, slowing down the flow at the door.
- **Regression result**: Specifically for number of recorded activities ($\beta_{BS}=-0.017$) this is a negative estimate, meaning that as more passengers board at a stop, the average boarding interval per passenger slightly decreases. In other words, boarding becomes marginally more efficient in larger groups — consistent with a "flow saturation" effect where passengers move in a more established rhythm when the queue is long.

#### Finding correlating to bus model configuration and alighting time

- According to distributions of alighting interval, it was found that the average alighting time passenger varies from 1.26s to 1.78s.
- Overall the average alighting time (1.48s) is about 0.30s shorter than the average boarding time (1.78s).
- **Negative relationship:** Higher on-board occupancy **reduces** alighting time per passenger
  ($\alpha_{Oc} = −0.082 s/pax$), meaning a more crowded bus leads to **faster alighting**.
- **Statistically significant:** The result is confirmed by a p-value of 0.000 and
  t-statistic of −4.41, indicating the effect is highly reliable and not due to chance.
- **Contrast with boarding:** This finding is the **opposite** of the occupancy effect on-boarding time, where higher occupancy *increases* boarding time (+0.340 s/pax) due to in-vehicle friction.
- **Behavioral explanations proposed:**
  - Passengers feel **discomfort from crowding**, motivating them to exit more quickly.
  - **Social pressure** from other passengers waiting to alight creates a push-through
    effect that accelerates the alighting flow.
- **Practical implication:** A crowded bus does **not** slow down alighting — crowding conditions may instead act as a behavioral stimulus that encourages passengers to exit faster, which is a fundamentally different dynamic from the boarding process.

#### Modelling passenger activity time for restricted flows Section 5

- *Restricted flow* in this study refers to specific boarding/alighting pattern where the boarding process is impeded or delayed because the bus is heavily occupied before it stops at a station. 
- Restricted flow was identified shown in Panel 4 in Fig 7 in this study, where high on-board occupancy delays boarding until enough passengers have alighted.
- *Critical occupancy* was also introduced in this study which refers to the on-board passenger **threshold** below which boarding can actually begin freely (63% total capacity of the bus suggested in this study)
- Passenger activity time is determined by three variables, i.e. the number of passengers boarding $B$, alighting $A$, and on-board $On$. 
- This study proposed a new model as Model III that considers both boarding/alighting dynamics and the interactions of on-board passengers shown below:

$$
Ac = \max \left\lbrace (B-1) \cdot b + \max(On - Cr,\ 0) \cdot a,\ (A-1) \cdot a \right\rbrace
$$

- It is identified from the proposed model that the authors did not consider that alighting process is not blocked by crowding meaning that people can always get off.

#### Comparison Model II and Model III

<u>Boarding Parameters</u>

- **Critical Occupancy ($\gamma$) is the dominant parameter in Model III**
  - Estimate: **0.633**, t-stat: **381.11**, p < 0.001
  - The largest t-statistic of any parameter in either model
  - Confirms that buses must be below ~63% capacity before boarding flows freely
  - Statistically validates the entire conceptual foundation of Model III

- **Raw occupancy ($\beta_{Oc}$) becomes insignificant once $\gamma$ is introduced**
  - Model II: $\beta_{Oc} = +0.359$ s/pax, p < 0.001 *(significant)*
  - Model III: $\beta_{Oc} = -0.001$ s/pax, p = 0.616 *(not significant)*
  - $\gamma$ fully absorbs the friction effect of crowding, making the direct
    occupancy term redundant in Model III

- **Model II inflates the double decker boarding effect ($\beta_D$)**
  - Model II: $\beta_D = +0.332$ s/pax, p < 0.001
  - Model III: $\beta_D = +0.080$ s/pax, p = 0.192 *(not significant)*
  - Model III estimate is consistent with the standalone Table 3 result (0.047 s/pax)
  - Model II incorrectly attributes restricted flow delay to the double decker effect
    — a sign of model misspecification

- **Step entrance ($\beta_S$) consistently slows boarding in both models**
  - Model II: $\beta_S = +0.186$ s/pax, p < 0.001
  - Model III: $\beta_S = +0.167$ s/pax, p < 0.001
  - ~0.17 s added per boarding passenger on step-entrance buses
  - Low-floor buses offer a reliable boarding time advantage

- **Diminishing marginal boarding effect ($\beta_{Bs}$) is consistent across both models**
  - Model II: $\beta_{Bs} = -0.016$ s/pax², p < 0.001
  - Model III: $\beta_{Bs} = -0.010$ s/pax², p < 0.001
  - Average boarding time per passenger decreases as total boarding demand increases
  - Larger crowds self-organize into a more stable and efficient boarding flow


<u>Alighting Parameters</u>

- **Double decker ($\alpha_D$) significantly and consistently delays alighting**
  - Model II: $\alpha_D = +0.235$ s/pax, p < 0.001
  - Model III: $\alpha_D = +0.285$ s/pax, p < 0.001
  - Most consistent finding across all analyses in the paper
  - Passengers wait upstairs until full stop before descending narrow stairs

- **Occupancy has opposite signs for alighting ($\alpha_{Oc}$) between the two models**
  - Model II: $\alpha_{Oc} = +0.329$ s/pax, p < 0.001 *(positive — counterintuitive)*
  - Model III: $\alpha_{Oc} = -0.371$ s/pax, p < 0.001 *(negative — consistent with Table 5)*
  - Model II misattributes restricted flow friction to the alighting process
  - Model III recovers the correct negative relationship: higher occupancy
    creates pressure that speeds up alighting, consistent with the standalone model

- **Diminishing marginal alighting effect ($\alpha_{As}$) is consistent across both models**
  - Model II: $\alpha_{As} = -0.023$ s/pax², p < 0.001
  - Model III: $\alpha_{As} = -0.012$ s/pax², p < 0.001
  - Average alighting time per passenger also decreases as alighting demand increases

- **Step entrance ($\alpha_S$) has a smaller but significant effect on alighting**
  - Model II: $\alpha_S = +0.087$ s/pax, p < 0.001
  - Model III: $\alpha_S = +0.075$ s/pax, p < 0.001
  - Step entrance adds less time to alighting than to boarding

<u>Overall Model Comparison</u>

- **Model III has slightly lower R² but produces more reliable estimates**
  - Model II R² = 0.870 vs. Model III R² = 0.844
  - Despite marginally lower variance explained, Model III parameters are
    correctly signed, physically interpretable, and consistent with standalone models
  - A structurally correct model with interpretable parameters is preferred
    over a higher R² achieved through misspecified occupancy effects

Notation Table

| Symbol              | Description                                                                                                                                                 |
|---------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| `Ac`                | **Total passenger activity time** — the interval between the first and last boarding/alighting tap at a stop (seconds). Excludes door open/close time.       |
| `B`                 | **Number of boarding passengers** — total passengers getting on the bus at the stop.                                                                        |
| `A`                 | **Number of alighting passengers** — total passengers getting off the bus at the stop.                                                                      |
| `On`                | **On-board passengers before the stop** — number of passengers already inside the bus when it arrives. Determines whether restricted flow occurs.            |
| `Cr`                | **Critical occupancy** — the on-board threshold below which boarding can begin freely. Defined as `Cr = γ · Cap`, where `γ ≈ 0.633` and `Cap` is total vehicle capacity. |
| `b`                 | **Average boarding time per passenger** (s/pax) — time per boarding activity, incorporating bus type, entrance type, and occupancy effects.                  |
| `a`                 | **Average alighting time per passenger** (s/pax) — time per alighting activity, incorporating bus type, entrance type, and occupancy effects.                |
| `(B − 1)`           | **Recorded boarding intervals** — because smart card data captures inter-tap intervals, only `N − 1` intervals are observed for `N` passengers.              |
| `(A − 1)`           | **Recorded alighting intervals** — same data limitation as boarding; `N − 1` intervals for `N` alighting passengers.                                        |
| `max(On − Cr, 0)`   | **Excess occupancy / delay trigger** — the number of passengers that must alight before boarding can start. Equals `0` when `On ≤ Cr` (no restriction); equals `On − Cr` when `On > Cr` (restricted flow). |
| `max { … , … }`     | **Regime selector** — total stop time is the longer of the two processes: the (penalized) boarding side or the alighting side. The bus waits until both are complete. |

How the Equation Works

Equation (10) consolidates four real-world boarding/alighting patterns
into a single expression:

| Condition                        | `max(On − Cr, 0)` | Effective Formula                               |
|----------------------------------|-------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| `On ≤ Cr` (bus not overcrowded)  | `= 0`             | `Ac = max { (B−1)·b , (A−1)·a }`               |
| `On > Cr` (bus overcrowded)      | `= On − Cr`       | `Ac = max { (B−1)·b + (On−Cr)·a , (A−1)·a }`  |

- When the bus is **not overcrowded** (`On ≤ Cr`), the penalty term
  vanishes and Model III reduces to the conventional simultaneous
  boarding/alighting model (Model II).
- When the bus is **overcrowded** (`On > Cr`), an extra delay term
  `(On − Cr) · a` is added to the boarding side, representing the
  time alighting passengers need to clear the excess crowd before
  boarding can freely begin. This is the **restricted flow** condition
  unique to Model III.

---

Notes

> The same symbol `a` (average alighting time per passenger) is used
> in both the boarding delay term `max(On − Cr, 0) · a` and the
> alighting term `(A − 1) · a`. In the full regression implementation
> (Equation 14), the authors distinguish between `a′` (alighting time
> during the clearing phase, where occupancy is still above Cr) and
> `a` (alighting time for the full alighting process), as each is
> estimated under slightly different occupancy conditions.

#### Overview

Pedestrian logic defines the rules and processes governing how simulated passengers move within the simulation environment. Passengers are categorized into three types — **alighting**, **boarding**, and **staying** — each represented as a circle with a 0.4 m diameter (a standard proxy for body size). Each passenger type follows its own distinct logic to carry out the corresponding behavior. Full program flowcharts implemented in AnyLogic are provided in Appendix A of the paper.

---

#### 3.3.1 Pedestrian Logic of Alighting Passengers

Alighting passengers select the **nearest door** and move to the corresponding interior waiting area inside the carriage. They queue and wait there until the door opens. Once the door opens, all alighting passengers pass through the door line and exit onto the platform, where they proceed to the **platform sink line** and are removed from the simulation.

---

#### 3.3.2 Pedestrian Logic of Boarding Passengers

Boarding passengers first move to the **platform waiting lines closest to their intended boarding door**. Each waiting line has a maximum capacity of **3 passengers**; additional passengers queue in the wider platform waiting areas. The boarding process is sequenced after alighting: boarding passengers only begin to enter the carriage through the door line once the number of remaining alighting passengers in the corresponding interior zone drops to **N_r or fewer**. After boarding, passengers wait inside the corresponding interior waiting area.

---

#### 3.3.3 Pedestrian Logic of Staying Passengers

Staying passengers are generated at the **interior source line** of the carriage. If seats are available, they occupy them in order until all seats are filled. Remaining standing passengers choose random locations inside the carriage and stand there. When alighting or boarding passengers move nearby, staying passengers **slightly shift their position** to yield space to the moving passengers.

---

#### Key Takeaways

- Passenger movement is governed by type-specific rules that reflect real-world queuing and yielding behavior.
- The boarding process is explicitly **conditioned on the alighting process** via a threshold parameter N_r, capturing the "alight-first, board-second" norm.
- Staying passengers play a passive but interactive role, dynamically adjusting positions to accommodate passenger flow.
- The model is implemented in **AnyLogic** using the Social Force Model (SFM) as the underlying movement engine.
