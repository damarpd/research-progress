## Boarding and alighting-related studies focusing policy intervention

- [Qu et al (2019)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.physa.2019.121075) mentioned the categories of factors affecting the boarding and alighting process:
    - Train and platform facility environment: platform interface design, height difference between platform and vehicle floor, the door width, and stepping gap.
    - Human behavior: time pressure, boarding group size, boarding ratio, queuing discipline, competitive, and individual desired speed.

### Train facility environment

- [Seriani and Fernandez, (2015)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.trc.2015.02.003) tested various scenarios, including the placement of **vertical handrail** and flow management of the passenger (specifically tested between **one-way door** separation between alighting and boarding, and **two-way door** which could be used for both alighting and boarding) with simulation and experimental tested which assessed by evaluation metrics, one of them is **Passenger Service Time (in sec)**.

**Summary of Section 3.1 — Time Headway Distribution**


---

**1. Objective**

Section 3.1 investigates the statistical nature of time headways between consecutive passengers
passing through the train door during the boarding and alighting process. The goal is to understand
flow fluctuations, clogs, and the variability of individual passenger behavior at the bottleneck.

---

**2. Key Notation**

| Symbol | Description |
|---|---|
| $t_{i,k}$ | Passage time of the $i$-th passenger with walking direction $k$ |
| $k = A$ | Alighting direction (exiting the train) |
| $k = B$ | Boarding direction (entering the train) |
| $i$ | Chronological rank of the passenger in the overall passage sequence |
| $i'$ | The previous passenger in the sequence **with the same walking direction** as passenger $i$ |
| $\Delta t$ | Time headway between two consecutive passengers |

---

**3. Equation (1) — Five Types of Adjacent Time Headway**

Time headway between chronologically consecutive passengers $i$ and $i-1$
(where $i-1$ is the immediately preceding passenger regardless of direction):

$$\Delta t_i^1 = t_{i,k} - t_{i-1,k'}$$

$$\Delta t_i^2 = t_{i,A} - t_{i-1,A} \quad \text{(Alight after Alight)}$$

$$\Delta t_i^3 = t_{i,A} - t_{i-1,B} \quad \text{(Alight after Board)}$$

$$\Delta t_i^4 = t_{i,B} - t_{i-1,A} \quad \text{(Board after Alight)}$$

$$\Delta t_i^5 = t_{i,B} - t_{i-1,B} \quad \text{(Board after Board)}$$

**Distinction between the five types:**

- **$\Delta t^1$** — General headway: no direction filter; measures the raw time gap between any
  two consecutive passengers in the mixed sequence.
- **$\Delta t^2$** — Both passengers alight (A→A): captures the spacing within the alighting group
  when they follow each other directly.
- **$\Delta t^3$** — Passenger $i$ alights, passenger $i-1$ boarded (B→A transition): captures the
  delay when flow direction switches from boarding to alighting.
- **$\Delta t^4$** — Passenger $i$ boards, passenger $i-1$ alighted (A→B transition): captures the
  delay when flow direction switches from alighting to boarding.
- **$\Delta t^5$** — Both passengers board (B→B): captures the spacing within the boarding group
  when they follow each other directly.

> **Key finding:** $\Delta t^3$, $\Delta t^4$, and $\Delta t^5$ are approximately equal on average,
> while the **Board–Board** headway ($\Delta t^5$) is notably the largest, making it the most
> important factor in overall process efficiency.

---

**4. Equation (2) — Same-Direction Time Headway**

Time headway between passenger $i$ and the previous passenger $i'$ travelling in the **same direction**
(intervening passengers of the opposite direction are skipped):

$$\Delta t_{si}^A = t_{i,A} - t_{i',A} \quad \text{(Alighting-only sequence headway)}$$

$$\Delta t_{si}^B = t_{i,B} - t_{i',B} \quad \text{(Boarding-only sequence headway)}$$

**Distinction from Equation (1):**

| Feature | Eq. (1) | Eq. (2) |
|---|---|---|
| Reference passenger | Immediately prior in **mixed** sequence ($i-1$) | Immediately prior in **same-direction** sub-sequence ($i'$) |
| Intervening opposite-direction passengers | **Included** in the gap | **Skipped over** |
| Purpose | Raw bottleneck flow intermittency | Pure within-group movement rhythm |
| Expected headway magnitude | Smaller (closer chronological neighbors) | Larger (spans over opposite-direction interleavings) |

---

**5. Data Overview**

- **255** boarding and alighting processes extracted from **7** Beijing metro stations.
- Stations: FXM (74), HLG (38), HY (26), XZM (59), GM (32), ZGC (22), TTY.
- Observation period: Morning peak hours, 07:00–10:00, April 10, 2018.
- Cut-off threshold: $\Delta t > 4$ s removed as abnormal data.
- **Average general time headway** ($\Delta t^1$) $\approx 0.68$ s across all stations.

---

**6. Probability Density Function (PDF) of Time Headway**

- The PDF of time headway follows a **positively skewed distribution**:
  - Rises quickly to a peak at approximately **0.8 s**.
  - Declines gradually, forming a **long right tail** toward larger values.
- The PDF **peak value** (≈ 0.8 s) represents the most probable time headway —
  the most common spacing passengers naturally maintain when passing through the door.
- **Higher PDF value** at a given interval → that gap duration occurs **more frequently**.
- **Lower PDF value** at a given interval → that gap duration occurs **less frequently**.

**Boarding vs. Alighting PDF comparison:**

| Characteristic | Alighting ($\Delta t^{sA}$) | Boarding ($\Delta t^{sB}$) |
|---|---|---|
| PDF shape | Narrower, concentrated at smaller headways | Broader, more dispersive |
| Long tail | Less pronounced | More pronounced (tail in [2 s, 4 s]) |
| Urgency level | Higher (stronger time pressure) | Lower |
| Interpretation | More uniform, rapid flow | More heterogeneous, irregular spacing |

> **Reason:** Alighting passengers face greater time pressure — missing the stop forces them to
> travel back from the next station ($t_2 \gg t_1$), so they move more urgently and consistently.

---

**7. Complementary Cumulative Distribution Function (CCDF)**

- Also known as the **survival function**.
- The tail of the CCDF can be fitted by **power-law** or **stretched exponential** distributions.
- Power-law tail compatibility confirmed with $p$-values $\geq 0.05$ (Clauset et al. method).
- The **Board–Board** headway ($\Delta t^5$) stands out as consistently the largest among all types,
  confirming that consecutive boarding passengers create the most significant flow delays.

---

**8. Key Findings Summary**

- Average time headway is approximately **0.68 s**, consistent across all observed stations.
- The PDF of time headway is **positively skewed** with a **power-law tail**.
- Alighting passengers have **smaller and more consistent** headways than boarding passengers
  due to greater time pressure.
- The **Board–Board** headway ($\Delta t^5$) is the dominant factor affecting process efficiency.
- Direction-transition headways ($\Delta t^3$, $\Delta t^4$) are comparable to Board–Board,
  suggesting that direction switching does not cause significantly extra delay beyond same-direction
  boarding flow.

**Summary of Section 3.2 — Activity Sequence Pattern and Burst Size**


---

**1. Objective**

Section 3.2 quantitatively characterizes the **crowd activity pattern** and **flow interruption
behavior** during the boarding and alighting process. Two new concepts are introduced: the
**order degree** ($d_{order}$) and the **burst size** ($s$). These measures capture how well
passengers comply with the "first alight" rule and how continuously passengers flow through the
bottleneck, respectively.

---

**2. Key Notation**

| Symbol | Description |
|---|---|
| $M$ | Total number of alighting passengers in a process |
| $N$ | Total number of boarding passengers in a process |
| $M + N$ | Total number of passengers in the process |
| $a$ | Actual activity string sequence of a boarding and alighting process (e.g., "ABBAB…") |
| $b$ | Ideal (perfect) activity string sequence obeying the "first alight" rule (e.g., "AA…ABB…B") |
| $lev_{a,b}(i,j)$ | Levenshtein distance between strings $a$ and $b$ — the minimum number of edits to transform one into the other |
| $d_{order}$ | Order degree — a normalized measure of crowd compliance with the "first alight" rule |
| $s$ | Burst size — number of passengers passing uninterruptedly through the door in one direction |
| $\langle s \rangle$ | Average burst size of a boarding and alighting process |
| $\langle \Delta t^1 \rangle$ | Average general time headway of a boarding and alighting process |

---

**3. Order Degree: Concept and Equation**

Each boarding and alighting process is represented as a **string sequence** of letters A (alighting)
and B (boarding) in the order passengers pass through the door. For example, a process may produce
the sequence `"AABABBB"`.

The **ideal sequence** (perfect rule compliance) for $M$ alighting and $N$ boarding passengers is:

$$\underbrace{AA\ldots A}_{M} \underbrace{BB\ldots B}_{N}$$

The **Levenshtein distance** $lev_{a,b}(i,j)$ measures the difference between the actual string
and the ideal string, defined recursively in Eq. (3) as:

$$lev_{a,b}(i,j) =
\begin{cases}
\max(i,j) & \text{if } \min(i,j) = 0 \\
\min
\begin{cases}
lev_{a,b}(i-1,j) + 1 \\
lev_{a,b}(i,j-1) + 1 \\
lev_{a,b}(i-1,j-1) + \mathbf{1}_{(a_i \neq b_j)}
\end{cases}
& \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}$$

The **order degree** is then defined in Eq. (4) as:

$$d_{order} = 1 - \frac{lev_{a,b}(i,j)}{M + N}$$

- Range: $0 \leq d_{order} \leq 1$
- **$d_{order} = 1$**: Perfect compliance — all alighting passengers exited before any boarding
  passenger entered.
- **$d_{order} = 0$**: Maximum disorder — the actual sequence is as far from the ideal as possible.
- **Average observed value**: approximately **0.8** across all Beijing metro stations, indicating
  that most passengers broadly follow the "first alight" rule.

> **Note:** In the special case where there are only boarding or only alighting passengers,
> the sequence is always perfectly ordered (e.g., "AAAA" or "BBBB"), and $d_{order} = 1$
> by definition. The order degree concept is therefore meaningful only for mixed-flow processes.

---

**4. Burst Size: Concept**

A **burst** is defined as an uninterrupted cluster of passengers from the same direction passing
consecutively through the train door. The **burst size** $s$ counts how many passengers pass
without interruption before a passenger from the opposite direction intervenes.

To calculate burst size accurately in a bi-directional flow, walking direction is used to separate
bursts, and passengers at the very beginning and end of each process are excluded. A critical
threshold time interval is used to define whether a flow is "uninterrupted" or has been broken by
a clog.

> **Key property:** Burst size monotonically increases as the threshold time interval grows, because
> a larger allowed gap means more passengers can pass before a clog is counted.

**Summary of Section 3.3 — Behavior under Time Pressure and Dwell Time Estimation**

---

**1. Objective**

Section 3.3 investigates how **time pressure** affects individual passenger behavior and the
overall crowd activity pattern during the boarding and alighting process. A new measurable
indicator — the **time gap** ($t_{gap}$) — is introduced to quantify time pressure. The section
also proposes a **Monte Carlo simulation-based approach** to estimate train dwell time using the
behavioral factors identified in Sections 3.1 and 3.2.

---

**2. Background: Time Pressure in Metro Stations**

During peak hours, congestion on both platforms and inside trains forces many passengers to
wait for multiple trains before boarding. The longer a passenger has waited, the stronger the
**time pressure** they experience — the urgency to complete their trip on time. Under high time
pressure, passengers transition from conservative (patient, rule-following) behavior to radical
(impatient, competitive) behavior, which can lead to serious conflicts at the train door.

The asymmetry of consequences of boarding and alighting passengers further amplifies this dynamic:

| Passenger Type | Consequence of Missing the Door |
|---|---|
| Boarding passenger | Waits for the next train at the same station (cost: $t_1$) |
| Alighting passenger | Must travel to the next station and return (cost: $t_2$) |

Since $t_2 \gg t_1$, alighting passengers consistently experience **greater time pressure** and
therefore move more urgently and consistently than boarding passengers.

---

**3. Key Notation**

| Symbol | Description |
|---|---|
| $t_{first}^B$ | Passage time of the **first boarding** passenger through the door |
| $t_{first}^A$ | Passage time of the **first alighting** passenger through the door |
| $t_{gap}$ | Time gap between the first boarding and first alighting passenger |
| $T_{dwell}$ | Estimated train dwell time |
| $\langle \Delta t \rangle$ | Average time headway across the entire boarding and alighting process |
| $N$ | Total number of boarding and alighting passengers |
| $d_{order}$ | Order degree of the process |
| $\langle s \rangle$ | Average burst size of the process |

---

**4. Definition of Time Gap**

The **time gap** $t_{gap}$ is defined as:

$$t_{gap} = t_{first}^B - t_{first}^A$$

- If $t_{gap} > 0$: the first boarding passenger entered **after** the first alighting passenger
  exited — passengers partially followed the "first alight" rule.
- If $t_{gap} = 0$: both groups started simultaneously.
- If $t_{gap} < 0$: the first boarding passenger entered **before** the first alighting passenger
  exited — the "first alight" rule was violated from the very start.

The time gap serves as a proxy for how much time pressure boarding passengers were under at the
moment the train door opened. A larger positive $t_{gap}$ means boarding passengers were more
patient and waited longer for alighting passengers to clear.

>   $d_{order} = 0.85$.

**6. Practical Application: Dwell Time Estimation**

The dwell time $T_{dwell}$ is approximately estimated as:

$$T_{dwell} = \langle \Delta t \rangle \cdot N$$

where $\langle \Delta t \rangle$ is the average time headway and $N$ is the total number of
boarding and alighting passengers.

However, because $\langle \Delta t \rangle$ is itself shaped by the **nonlinear interactions**
between time headway distribution, burst size, order degree, and time pressure, dwell time
**cannot be obtained from a simple closed-form formula**. The authors propose a **Monte Carlo
simulation** approach to estimate dwell time, following these steps:

1. Set the numbers of boarding ($N_B$) and alighting ($N_A$) passengers, and generate an
   activity pattern.
2. Randomly generate a time gap $t_{gap}$ between the first boarding and first alighting passenger.
3. Based on the flow ratio and $t_{gap}$, randomly generate the order degree $d_{order}$ and
   average burst size $\langle s \rangle$ according to the empirical relationships identified in
   Sections 3.1 and 3.2.
4. Generate a series of activity patterns satisfying the given $d_{order}$ and $\langle s \rangle$.
5. Repeat steps 2–4 $n$ times to obtain the distribution of average time headway and then
   calculate the estimated dwell time $T_{dwell}$.

---

**7. Key Findings Summary**

- The **time gap** $t_{gap} = t_{first}^B - t_{first}^A$ is a practical and measurable indicator
  of time pressure and crowd orderliness.
- A **negative time gap** (boarding starts before alighting) sharply reduces order degree to
  ~0.6 — the most disordered scenario.
- A **positive time gap** in the range **[5.40 s, 8.52 s]** is recommended to maintain a high
  order degree of approximately **$d_{order} = 0.85$**.
- Average burst size **linearly increases** with time gap in the range
  $0.3\ \text{s} \leq t_{gap} \leq 5.8\ \text{s}$, confirming that more patient boarding
  behavior produces larger, cleaner bursts and better flow efficiency.
- Dwell time estimation requires a **Monte Carlo simulation** approach due to the complex,
  nonlinear relationships between time headway, burst size, order degree, and time pressure.
- The time gap finding provides metro operators with an actionable flow control strategy:
  **physically or visually delaying boarding passengers** for approximately 5–9 seconds after
  the doors open can substantially improve safety, order, and throughput.
