## Discrete Approach in Pedestrian Modeling

### A Joint Model of Pedestrian Movers and Stayers in Urban Public Spaces
- A study from [Fujiwara, K., et al. (n.d.)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5163637)
- **Rationale**: Pedestrian model which was developed by considered the interactions between <mark>movers and stayers</mark> since most of the studies focus on **movers**.
- Additionally, by considering mover-stayer competition can lead to unexpected behavioral phenomena which can contradict the implications from the existing pedestrian fundamental diagrams [(Vanumu et al. 2017)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12544-017-0264-6).
- This study consider not only internal positive and negative interactions among movers and stayers, but also external negative between movers and stayers (external means interactions across pedestrian type ~movers and stayers).
- Interaction within same pedestrian types (e.g. movers ~ movers or stayers ~ stayers) -> internal interaction; otherwise external
- An interaction is considered positive if it increases the utilities of movers or stayers, and negative if it decreases them.

- **Research objective**: to examine how public space design influences pedestrian behavior and overall performance.
- This study argued that modeling behavior in a <mark>continuous space</mark> is crucial because pedestrian interactions (i.e. collision avoidance) occur in continuous space (where the distance between pedestrian is a key).

<u>Pedestrian Model with Movers and Stayer Interactions</u>

- **Necessity of continuous space modeling**: It is critical to model pedestrian behavior in continuous space because <mark>fundamental interactions</mark>, *such as collision avoidance, are dictated by the physical distance between individuals*.
- **Limitations of existing continuous models**: While established models (e.g., [Hoogendoorn and Bovy 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0191-2615(03)00007-9); [Robin et.al.,2009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.trb.2008.06.010)) describe behavior in continuous space, they encounter significant challenges when trying to calculate a unified performance indicator for a space shared by both movers and stayers 

- **Inadequacy of traditional metrics**: Metrics like travel speed or collision frequency are effective for evaluating the performance of movers, but they are unsuitable for stayers, whose goals and activities differ from those of travelers.
- **Requirement for a consistent evaluation framework**: To effectively design and appraise urban public spaces, an alternative framework is required that can consistently derive performance indicators for both movers and stayers simultaneously.

> Hence, this study proposed the consumer surplus to evaluate the performance of urban public spaces for both movers and stayers

- **The conflict between continuous space and discrete modeling**: Pedestrian must exist in continuous space given the <mark>fundamental interactions</mark> among pedestrians, while holding consumer surplus as an output of performance indicator of the study (requiring dynamic discrete choice framework).

- **The Bridge -> Stochastic State Transition**: The transition from the current continuous position to the next is stochastic (probabilistic) for movers. The action chosen in the discrete grid determines the "representative" destination, but a transition probability function then determines exactly where in the continuous space the pedestrian ends up.

- Below the overall value function $V_t(s_t)$ following Bellman equation:

$$V_t(s_t) = \max_{a \in A(s_t)} (v_t(a|s_t) + EV_t(s_t, a) + \mu \varepsilon_t(a))$$

- where the sum of deterministic component of instantaneous utility $v_t(a|s_t)$ is determined as follows:

$$v_t(a|s_t) = \begin{cases} 
\boldsymbol{\beta}^{mo}x_t^{mo}(a|s_t) + \gamma^{mo}w_t^{mo}(a|s_t) & \text{if } a \text{ is move action} \\ 
\beta^{st}x_t^{st}(a|s_t) + \gamma^{st}\mathbf{w}_t^{st}(a|s_t) & \text{if } a \text{ is stay action} 
\end{cases}$$

- **The future component of decision-making**-> the model assumes pedestrians are rational agents who make decisions to maximize their total benefit. 
- When a pedestrian at state $s_{t}$ chooses an action $a$, they consider two things:
    - Instantaneous utility: the immediate benefit or cost of that specific action
    - Expected value of the value function: the total expected utility they will gain from all *subsequent* steps after that action is completed

- The expected value of the value function is expressed as:

$$EV_t(s_t, a) = \int_{\check{s}} \left( q_t(\check{s}|s_t,a) \overline{V}_{t+1}(\check{s}) \right) dq_t(\check{s}|s_t,a)$$

## Others Significant Approach

### A Pedestrian Model Considering Anticipatory Behavior for Capacity Evaluation

- A study from [Asano, Iryo & Kuwahara (2009)](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-1-4419-0820-9_28)
- This study argues that continuous model seem to be able to reproduce pedestrian movement, but the movements of the pedestrians are not directly related to decision-making processes of pedestrians.
- **Rationale**: besides the decision processes of *individual pedestrians*, the decision processes of *pedestrians interacting with each other* should be considered to reproduce pedestrian flow in congested situations. 
- To avoid collision or stacking, pedestrians may anticipate other pedestrians' behavior in *future* to determine the order of priority without assuming any external rule.
- **Nash Equilibrium** concept was adopted to this pedestrian modeling, as two or more players will try to choose strategy to maximize their utility.
- Though this proposed pedestrian game concept (pedestrian going and waiting to avoid collision or waiting forever) is too simple, but incorporating the concept of game theory into the model should be essential in describing the decision process of pedestrian's interactions.
- A proposed two-player game is following:

|Strategy of Player 1/Strategy of Player 2|Going|Waiting|
|-----------------------------------------|-----|-------|
|Going|(-100,-100)|(+10,+1)|
|Waiting|(+1,+10)|(-10,-10)|

<u>Ideal condition</u>

- **Ideal condition** -> proposed a *pedestrian flow game under perfect information* assumption where all pedestrians can anticipate perfectly how and where other pedestrians will move in future until all pedestrians finish their trip (reaching final destination).
- Limitations of the "Perfect Information" Framework: While theoretically logical, the pedestrian flow game with perfect information fails to accurately represent real-world pedestrian behavior.
- Flawed Assumption: This framework assumes pedestrians study and anticipate others' movements before walking. While this works for vehicular traffic where drivers learn routes and congestion patterns over time, it does not apply to pedestrians.
- Unpredictability of Pedestrian Paths: Unlike road traffic, **pedestrian trajectories rarely repeat, even in the same location and under similar congestion levels, making prior learning impossible**.
- Real-time Estimation: Pedestrians likely adapt their movements based on others while walking rather than planning ahead. However, these estimations are short-lived due to evolving decisions and visual constraints.
- Proposed Solution: Future modeling of pedestrian behavior will utilize a "rolling horizon" concept to address these dynamic complexities.

<u>Rolling horizon game</u>

- **Rolling horizon game** -> this model takes account of the *limited ability* of pedestrians to collect information and anticipating others' future movements (i.e, limitations of eyesight).
- It is reasonable to assume that pedestrians can anticipate others movements for a shorter time horizon (let say just in seconds) -> this reflect real-world behavior where intentions are often communicated through body language or eye contact only for the immediate future.
- **Rolling horizon game**: is a normal form game within a limited time horizon and visibility.
- The intention of moving within the time horizon is represented by an intended trajectory
- The intended trajectory may not be the same as the actual trajectory, because it may be modified by the pedestrian at every time step due to changes of circumstances such as the appearance of new pedestrians and so on.
