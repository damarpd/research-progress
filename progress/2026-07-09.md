## Literature review

### On distinction between crowding and density

- A study from [Stokols (1972)](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0032706)

#### Content
- The author points out a major flaw in existing research: the terms "density" and "crowding" are often used interchangeably. He argues that this confusion leads researchers to view crowding only through a spatial lens, ignoring the social and personal dimensions that actually trigger the experience.
- Stokols explains that density (limited space) is a necessary starting point for crowding, but it is not enough on its own to cause the experience. He notes that people might not even notice spatial constraints if their activities don’t require much coordination, if they are friendly with those around them, or if they are used to living in tight quarters.
- To turn physical density into the aversive experience of "crowding," certain factors must be present:
    - Social interference: Problems coordinating tasks or competing with others for space make spatial limits more obvious
    - Personal traits: People who lack experience with limited space or who possess traits like impatience or aggressiveness are more susceptible to feeling crowded
- The author clarifies that his discussion focuses on situations where space is not so scarce that it causes immediate biological harm (like extreme heat or stuffiness). He acknowledges that in cases of acute physical discomfort, density alone might be enough to induce a crowding experience without needing social triggers.
- Stokols defines crowding as a motivational state. It is the result of a person perceiving a disparity between the space they want or need and the space actually available. This state drives the person to try to fix the situation, either by getting more space or by adjusting their social environment.
- The author outlines how people react to feeling crowded based on their circumstances:
    - Behavioral response: If possible, a person will physically leave the crowded area to gain more space. 
    - Perceptual/Cognitive response: If they cannot leave (due to physical or social rules), they might adjust their mindset by lowering their standards for what "adequate space" is or trying to cooperate better with the group.
- The author critiques recent experiments that failed to find significant effects when they only changed room size. He suggests these studies were unsuccessful because they didn't account for social and personal variables. He argues that future research must manipulate all three dimensions—spatial, social, and personal—to truly understand the determinants of crowding. 
- In the final section, Stokols calls for research that treats crowding as a measurable experience. He suggests using a variety of tools, including:
    - Behavioral measures (how people try to get more space).
    - Subjective reports (how restricted the person feels).
    - Observational indexes (signs of tension like reduced eye contact or aggression).
    - Physiological indicators (stress levels).
